A friend asked me about the move recently. Five questions, all fair:

- Tell me more about it.
- How's the experience?
- How are you organizing your time?
- How are you going to pay rent?
- How did the company take it?

I've been answering these in DMs and over coffee for weeks. Let me just write it down once.

## The Job I Left

For three years I was at Sats Capital, a Bitcoin-only cashback platform. Think Méliuz or Livelo, but every reward goes back to you in BTC.

I started as an intern. By the end I was Lead Developer with acting-CTO scope: full ownership of the tech, the architecture, the hires, the call when something broke at 3am.

I owe my CEO a real debt for that ride. He hired the intern version of me and let me grow into the actual job, which is rare. The product was mine to shape. The team was mine to build. I had latitude most senior engineers don't get even after a decade.

So why leave?

A few things stacked up.

The salary was below market. That's the easy one. I had equity, but the vesting locked me for another three years. The product was mine in spirit, more than on paper.

Bigger: I was shipping under tight deadlines that increasingly demanded I lean on AI to hit dates. I'm not anti-AI. I use it. But there's a version of "productive" where you stop reading what you ship, and I felt myself drifting toward that line.

I'm wired badly for that. If something is hard, I want to run at it, not route around it. If I can't walk something, I try to sprint. I've never figured out how to be lazy in work, and trying made me feel sick of myself.

The conflict wasn't with anyone there. It was inside me.

## The Trigger

While I was still at Sats Capital, I went to SatsConf, Brazil's biggest Bitcoin-only conference. That's where I started talking to Vinteum.

Vinteum is a Brazilian non-profit that funds developers to contribute to Bitcoin open source. Grants, fellowships, programs. I applied to BDL, the Bitcoin Developer Launchpad. Four months of intensive Bitcoin protocol training.

Every deliverable left me feeling like I knew nothing about how Bitcoin actually worked.

I'd ship something for BDL on a Sunday, then sit at my day job on Monday and feel the gap between the two. The Bitcoin code felt like the kind of work I'd write if no one was watching. The day job code felt like the kind of work I'd write because someone was.

I didn't apply to BDL planning to win a fellowship on the first try. I applied to start building a relationship with the people doing the work I respected most.

Then I got invited to the in-person phase in Florianópolis. Gave a lightning talk, and participated in btc++. Sat in rooms with people I'd been reading for years. That trip is the one I point to when I trace the decision back to a moment.

I met some of the smartest people I've ever been around: Edil, b10c, Nifty, Davidson, Lucas, Noctus, Johnny, Isaque, Joãozinho, and a long list more. For the first time I understood I had a lot left to learn if I wanted to keep up with them.

That's the kind of thing you can't unfeel.

## The Quieter Reason

Growing up, I always felt slightly out of step. The things that bothered me didn't seem to bother the people around me. And where I'm from, the state is usually the first thing people turn to when something's wrong. I never thought that way and I never had the vocabulary for why.

Bitcoin gave me the vocabulary. Libertarian thinking, applied cryptography, the long history of failed money. I read all of that and realized my instincts already had names. Other people had thought them through more carefully than I had, and there was a remedy at the end of it.

Meeting the Vinteum crowd was the second half of that. Not the ideas existing on a page, but people who organize their working lives around them. That's what closed the loop.

## How It's Actually Going

Mixed and good.

The honest answer: I haven't done anything big yet. Right now I'm reviewing PRs and writing end-to-end tests on flows I'm trying to understand. That's the work. It's slower than I want. It's also the right speed.

Part of what I'm doing is detoxing. I spent the last year-plus shipping with heavy AI assist, and the muscle that reads code carefully, without an LLM in the loop, atrophied more than I want to admit. I'm rebuilding it. Some days that means I read one file for two hours. That's progress, even when it doesn't feel like it.

The win is harder to put in a sentence: the work fulfills me. I study with intent. I don't outsource my ownership of what I ship. I belong to a community whose values track with mine. The fog I didn't know I was walking through is gone.

## How I Organize My Time

Everything goes in a vault.

I use Neovim like an org-mode setup. Every task I do produces a note, a repo, or both. Versioned, traceable, dated. If I can't find it later, I didn't really do it.

My calendar is CalDav, self-hosted on my VPS. I journal: feelings, wins, losses, what I'm working through. Accountability matters and I'm the only one watching most of the time.

I'm still in college. I'm finishing my degree at Universidade Católica de Salvador, which eats four to six hours a day Tuesday through Thursday. So the fellowship work fits around lectures and assignments, not the other way around.

I'm running BitDevs Salvador and the Liga Acadêmica de Blockchain at my university. I'm picking up applied cryptography on the side. None of this is optional in my head. It's all part of the same thing.

I'm not optimized. I'm slipping on workouts and I notice it. I still need to get better at protecting deep work from open browser tabs. But the framework is there.

## How I'm Paying Rent

Six-month fellowship, paid in BTC. Monthly reports.

You could read "monthly report" as a leash. I read it as accountability, and I want it. Any contract worth having has a way to verify the work.

It pays meaningfully more than my Lead Developer salary did. That part still feels weird to say out loud.

Before this I was OK but not comfortable. Nothing dramatic, no meaningful debt, but I worked hard for output that didn't always match the input, and I picked up freelances to keep cashflow steady. The fellowship ended that.

I've always thought money follows good work. I still think that. The catch is that "good work" has to be legible to whoever's signing the cheque, and most of the time the burden of making it legible, selling yourself, is on you. The fellowship is the first time my idea of good work and the cheque-signer's idea of good work actually point at the same thing.

Money isn't everything. Recognition, purpose, and the feeling of doing real work are better. But you can't photosynthesize, and pretending otherwise is how people quietly burn out of open source. The fellowship is what makes this version of my life sustainable.

## How the Company Took It

I'll keep this short out of respect (and because of my NDA).

The conversation with CEO was direct on both sides. He gave me my start. I told him the truth about why I was leaving and what I was leaving for. The relationship is intact.

If I tried to write more than that, I'd be performing.

## What I'd Tell Someone Considering This

Don't quit your day job for an idea of open source. Quit for a relationship with one: a project, a community, a set of people whose work you already respect and who already know your name a little.

I didn't apply to a fellowship. I applied to a program, did the work, showed up in person, gave the talk, and the fellowship came after. The order matters. Every step of that I could have stopped at, and the worst case was: I'd have learned a lot of Bitcoin protocol I didn't know.

Take the program. Go to the conference. Write the lightning talk.

The career change, if it happens, is downstream of those.
