I'll be direct: AI won't replace everyone.

The most advanced model you can run today still needs someone who knows what to ask, how to ask it, and what to do with the result. Without that, you've got an expensive text generator spitting out generic code.

AGI? Bullshit.
Pure marketing.
It's a narrative sold by companies that need to justify absurd valuations and billion-dollar funding rounds. Meanwhile, what we actually have is a glorified autocomplete that, yes, is useful. But nowhere close to thinking on its own.

## What AI actually does

What happens in practice is simple: you can produce more in less time.

That's it.

Not necessarily something better.
Not necessarily something elegant.
Not necessarily something you'd be proud to put in a PR.
But faster? Absolutely.

With AI I managed to run [a startup](https://satscapital.com.br/) entirely on my own: product, backend, infra... 
But that wasn't because of AI.

It was because **I knew how to use it**.
I know how to code.
I know *what* to code.
I know how to use AI as a tool, not as a crutch.

A product person, designer, PO, can't be as productive as I am, or as any technical person is, using AI. Because the tool amplifies what you already know. If you know nothing, it amplifies nothing.

## Is code a commodity, and are workers just assets?

And this is where it gets personal.

I'm Not afraid of being replaced. I think that's impossible in my case.
But because I feel zero pleasure injecting prompts into a terminal and just collecting the result after burning $100 USD in AI credits.

This isn't work.

There's no joy in this kind of development.
You don't learn anything new.
You don't face a real problem.
You don't feel that satisfaction of debugging something for hours and finally understanding what was wrong.
You just... ask and receive.

It's like ordering delivery every day. It works. It fills you up. 
But you're not cooking. You're not building a skill. You're consuming.

## The business mindset that's killing the craft

This race for productivity comes from a purely business-driven view of our work: **software is expensive**.

AI makes you produce faster. Period.
That's what matters to whoever signs the check.

This behavior will be enforced by companies. Soon enough, anyone who doesn't use AI will be fired. Or you'll have to at least pretend you're using it. It doesn't matter if the code is good. It doesn't matter if it's maintainable. What matters is speed and cost reduction.

Is it sad? Yes. But it's reality.

## Why I'm making a change

All of this is why I'm transitioning to something different.

I'm moving toward more niche, low-level work. Environments where using AI is frowned upon. Not because people are luddites. Because the work there is about deeply understanding what you're building. Every line of code matters. Every design decision matters. You can't just generate 500 lines and start committing.

And you know what I notice every time I work like that? I feel joy again.

When you need to read a spec, understand a protocol, implement something from scratch and prove it works, there's no shortcut.
You need to **understand**. And understanding is what makes me love what I do.

> The one who works with his hands is a worker.
> The one who works with his hands and his mind is a craftsman.
> The one who works with his hands, his mind and his heart is an artist.
